Domestic Waste Management

Introduction

Domestic waste is a serious problem in all agglomerations of Mozambique due to a growth in population numbers and nonbiodegradable waste and the absence of sealed dumpsites.

  • Classification of solid waste components

  • Improper disposal of Domestic waste from industrial processes can lead to land pollution.

  • Organic Waste:
    • Waste is a major environmental issue that affects the quality of our surroundings, health, and the natural ecosystems. Waste can be classified into two primary types: organic and inorganic waste.

  • Innorganic Waste:
    • inorganic waste originates from chemical substances and materials that do not come from living beings, such as glass, plastic, and metal. The decomposition of inorganic waste is a slow process that can take thousands of years, making their recycling more complex.
  • Recyclable Waste:
    • The Waste which can be Recycled is called Recyclable Waste.
  • Radioactive Waste:
    • Radioactive waste is broadly classified into low-level waste (LLW), such as paper, rags, tools, clothing, which contain small amounts of mostly short-lived radioactivity, intermediate-level waste (ILW), which contains higher amounts of radioactivity and requires some shielding.
    Incineration of plastic waste in some industrial processes can release harmful pollutants into the air, contributing to air pollution and potential health hazards.

    preventive Measures

  • Reduce and Reuse: Encourage industries to minimize the use of single-use plastics and adopt reusable and sustainable alternatives.
  • Recycling:
    • Implement and promote effective recycling programs for industrial plastic waste to reduce the amount of plastic entering landfills and ecosystems.
  • Circular Economy :
    • Embrace a circular economy approach, where the lifecycle of plastic products is optimized through recycling, reusing, and reducing waste.
  • Regulations and Policies:
    • Implement and enforce strict regulations on the disposal and management of industrial plastic waste to ensure responsible practices.
  • Innovation and Research:
    • Invest in research and innovation to develop eco-friendly alternatives to traditional plastics and more sustainable industrial processes.

    Plastic Waste in Industrial Processes

    Learn about the sources of plastic waste in various industries and their environmental implications.

  • Production and Consumption: Raw Material Use: The production of plastic involves the extraction and processing of fossil fuels, such as oil and natural gas. This process contributes to environmental degradation and resource depletion.
    Single-Use Plastics: Industries often use plastic for packaging, leading to a large amount of single-use plastic waste. This contributes significantly to the plastic pollution problem.

  • Waste Generation:

  • Manufacturing Waste:
    • During the manufacturing of plastic products, waste is generated in the form of defective or excess materials. End-of-life Products: When plastic products reach the end of their life cycle, they often become waste, either through disposal or improper management. Recycling Challenges:
  • Complexity of Plastic Recycling:
    • Many plastics are challenging to recycle due to variations in composition, color, and the presence of additives.
  • Limited Recycling Infrastructure:
    • Inadequate recycling infrastructure in many regions hinders the efficient collection and processing of plastic waste.
  • Pollution and Environmental Impact:
    • Land and Water Pollution: Improper disposal of plastic waste leads to pollution of land and water bodies. Plastics can take hundreds of years to degrade, causing long-term environmental damage.
  • Microplastics:
    • Over time, larger plastic items break down into smaller particles known as microplastics, which can enter ecosystems and potentially harm aquatic life.
  • Health Concerns:
  • Toxic Additives:
    • Some plastics contain additives that can be harmful to human health. When plastic waste degrades or incinerates, these additives may release toxins into the environment.

  • Legislation and Policies:
    • Governments and international organizations are implementing regulations to reduce single-use plastics, promote recycling, and encourage the responsible management of plastic waste.
  • Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR):
    • Some regions have adopted EPR programs, requiring producers to take responsibility for the end-of-life management of their products, including proper disposal and recycling.

  • Biodegradable Plastics:
